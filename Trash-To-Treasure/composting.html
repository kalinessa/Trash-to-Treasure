What is Composting?

Through composting, organic materials like food scraps decompose into compost, a nutrient-rich material that can be applied to gardening and agriculture. Composting is a natural process of recycling food waste to improve soil.

Why Compost?

  • Over 63 million tons of food waste was produced in 2018, only 4.1% of which was composted. Over half of all food waste was sent to landfills (EPA).
  • Food waste in landfills generates large amounts of methane, a strong greenhouse gas (EPA). Composting greatly reduces methane emissions.
  • Composting reduces the need for fertilizers and pesticides while improving soil health, which is useful for restoring wetlands, forests, and habitats.

What to Compost

To make compost, you need water, air, carbon-rich materials like dry leaves and plant stalks (also called “browns”), and nitrogen-rich materials like food scraps (also called “greens”) (EPA). View the items that are compostable or not compostable below:

  • Food and vegetable scraps
  • Coffee grounds and paper filters
  • Crushed eggshells
  • Grass clippings
  • Small pieces of cardboard and paper
  • Leaves
  • Plant stalks and twigs
  • Meat and bones
  • Cheese and dairy products
  • Oils and greases
  • Cooked food
  • Colored or glossy paper
  • Produce stickers
  • Plastic

How to Compost

Backyard Composting

  1. Store food scraps (“greens”) in a closed container, and set aside a space outside for leaves and twigs (“browns”).
  2. Get a bin to store your compost pile; it should be located close to a water source.
  3. Prepare composting ingredients by breaking food scraps into smaller pieces.
  4. Start the composting pile with a layer of browns, and then layer on greens and browns in an alternating order. There should be two to three times more browns than greens.
  5. Maintain the pile by occasionally turning and mixing the materials.
  6. Let the compost cure for four weeks when there are no visible food scraps and the pile no longer heats up after mixing.
